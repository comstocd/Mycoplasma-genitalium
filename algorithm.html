Non-Gonococcal Urethritis & Mycoplasma genitalium:
Step-by-Step Management Algorithm

First encounter through confirmed cure — outpatient primary care and urgent care settings
Outpatient NGU / Urethritis CDC STI Guidelines 2021 Comstock D, Gareca MG  ·  Guthrie Health System  ·  2026
Clinical Use Notice: This algorithm is intended as an educational reference for clinicians and trainees. It reflects CDC 2021 STI Treatment Guidelines, IUSTI/European 2016 guidelines, and current evidence-based practice. It does not substitute for clinical judgment. Individual patient circumstances, local resistance patterns, and institutional formulary should guide management decisions.
Step types Assessment Diagnostics Decision point Treatment Warning / avoid Counseling
1
Assessment
Separate true UTI from urethritis — before prescribing anything

In a sexually active person presenting with dysuria, the first clinical task is not to prescribe — it is to determine the correct diagnostic category. True cystitis and urethritis share symptoms but have different etiologies, workups, and treatments entirely.

🔵 Likely Urethritis
  • Sexually active, new or multiple partners
  • Urethral discharge (purulent or mucopurulent)
  • Symptoms after new partner or urethral instrumentation
  • UA normal or mildly abnormal, urine culture negative
  • In males: anatomically unusual for true ascending UTI

→ Proceed to Step 2 (STI workup)

🟡 Likely True UTI
  • Female, postmenopausal, or structural urological abnormality
  • Frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain
  • UA with pyuria AND bacteriuria
  • No new sexual exposure, no discharge

→ Standard UTI workup + culture-guided therapy

Note: In young men without structural abnormality, a UTI diagnosis warrants urology referral.

Key principle: In a sexually active young man with dysuria, urethritis is far more likely than true UTI. The reflex to prescribe TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin without a urine culture misses urethritis routinely and delays the correct diagnosis.

2
Diagnostics
Order the right tests at the first visit

First-catch urine (first 20–30 mL of stream) is preferred for all NAATs. Urethral swab is an acceptable alternative.

TestSpecimenNotes
GC / CT NAATFirst-catch urine or urethral swabMinimum standard. Universally available.
Trichomonas vaginalis NAATFirst-catch urineUnderdiagnosed in men. Common cause of persistent NGU. Do not omit.
M. genitalium NAATFirst-catch urineQuest and LabCorp offer this. Available at many reference labs. Order at first visit in all NGU and all recurrent presentations.
UrinalysisMidstream urinePyuria (≥10 WBC/hpf) supports urethritis. Absence does not exclude.
Urine cultureMidstream clean catchTo rule out true UTI. Provides baseline.
HIV, syphilis, HBV, HCVSerumCo-test at the same visit. Do not defer.
M. genitalium resistance genotyping (23S rRNA)Same NAAT specimenNot universally available. Where available, order at time of positive NAAT — not after first treatment failure.

Don't wait for results before initiating empiric treatment in symptomatic urethritis — but tailor follow-up management to the NAAT results when they return.

3
Treatment
Empiric treatment at the first encounter — the drug choice matters
⚠️ GC suspected (purulent discharge, exposure)
  • Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose (1 g if ≥150 kg)
  • Plus Doxycycline 100 mg BID × 7 days for chlamydia coverage
  • Per CDC 2021 STI guidelines
  • Treat empirically; do not wait for NAAT result
✅ NGU — GC not suspected
  • Doxycycline 100 mg BID × 7 days — preferred first-line
  • If T. vaginalis suspected: add Metronidazole 2 g single dose
🚫
Do not use Azithromycin 1 g as empiric monotherapy for NGU. This is a practice-changing update from prior guidelines. Azithromycin 1 g treats chlamydia effectively but delivers effective macrolide monotherapy to any co-existing undiagnosed M. genitalium, which is present in up to 38% of NGU cases. This is the primary iatrogenic driver of macrolide resistance in M. genitalium.

Why doxycycline is the right first choice: It covers chlamydia and has modest activity against M. genitalium. Critically, it does not select macrolide resistance, and it serves as a debulking agent — reducing bacterial load by ~2 logs — if moxifloxacin is needed later.

4
Counseling
Partner management and behavioral counseling — same visit, not deferred
Partner treatment
60-day lookback
All partners from the preceding 60 days (or most recent partner if no sexual contact in >60 days) should be evaluated and treated.
Expedited partner therapy
EPT — legal in most states
Prescribe or dispense treatment for the partner without the partner being seen. Recommended by CDC when partner evaluation is not feasible.
Abstinence window
7 days post-treatment
Abstain from sexual activity for 7 days after completing treatment AND until all partners are treated. Routinely under-counseled.

6
Treatment
Confirmed M. genitalium — recurrent or persistent: escalation algorithm

The doxycycline debulking → moxifloxacin sequence is a M. genitalium-specific regimen. Do not apply it empirically to undifferentiated NGU without a confirmed positive NAAT.

✅ Resistance genotyping available

Macrolide-susceptible (wild-type 23S rRNA):

  • Doxycycline 100 mg BID × 7d (debulking)
  • → Azithromycin 1 g day 1, then 500 mg days 2–4

Macrolide-resistant (A2058G/A2059G mutation):

  • Doxycycline 100 mg BID × 14d (debulking)
  • → Moxifloxacin 400 mg daily × 14d
  • Before prescribing moxifloxacin: see Step 7
🔵 No resistance testing available (US default)

Presume macrolide resistance (>50–64% US prevalence). Treat as macrolide-resistant:

  • Doxycycline 100 mg BID × 14d (debulking)
  • → Moxifloxacin 400 mg daily × 14d
  • Before prescribing moxifloxacin: see Step 7
⚠️
Do not escalate to moxifloxacin without confirming M. genitalium NAAT is positive. Fluoroquinolone resistance in M. genitalium is currently 6–11% in the US but rising. Every empiric moxifloxacin course in a patient without confirmed M. genitalium is a stewardship cost with no benefit.
7
Warning
Before every moxifloxacin prescription: supplement history + chelation counseling

Fluoroquinolones chelate polyvalent metal cations (Fe²⁺, Zn²⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) in the gastrointestinal lumen prior to absorption, forming insoluble complexes that are not bioavailable. The pharmacokinetic consequence is clinically significant:

AUC reduction
~39%
Area under the curve (total drug exposure) reduced by ~39% with mineral co-ingestion
Cmax reduction
~59%
Peak serum concentration reduced by ~59%. Moxifloxacin is concentration-dependent: AUC/MIC drives efficacy.
Clinical result
Treatment failure
Sub-inhibitory exposure = pharmacokinetic failure indistinguishable from microbiological resistance. Also selects for fluoroquinolone resistance.

Ask specifically about all of the following. Patients do not volunteer supplement use when asked about medications.

Multivitamins
One-A-Day, Centrum, GNC
Iron supplements
FeSO₄, ferrous gluconate
Zinc supplements
Zinc gluconate, zinc picolinate
Calcium supplements
Calcium carbonate, citrate
Magnesium
Mg oxide, glycinate, citrate
Antacids
Tums, Maalox, Pepto-Bismol
Sucralfate

Milk, yogurt, cheese

Counseling instruction: Discontinue ALL mineral-containing supplements and antacids for the full duration of moxifloxacin therapy. Complete abstinence is preferred. If a supplement is medically necessary (e.g., iron for iron-deficiency anemia), minimum separation is 4–6 hours — taken well after the antibiotic dose. Provide this in writing at dispensing.

8
Diagnostics
Test of cure — timing is not optional

M. genitalium NAAT detects nucleic acid, not viable organisms. Dead organism DNA persists in urethral secretions for 14–21 days after successful eradication. Obtaining TOC during this window produces unreliable results in either direction.

Days after completing antibiotics

Treatment ends
Day ~14–21
Minimum TOC
Day ~28

Day 42+
Too early — DNA from non-viable organisms may give false negative or false positive Reliable window — obtain TOC here
⚠️
Do not obtain TOC before 3 weeks post-treatment. A negative result at day 6 falsely reassures. A positive result in the same window may trigger an unnecessary additional antibiotic course — further selecting resistance. This case included a premature negative NAAT at day 6 post-moxifloxacin; the definitive confirmatory TOC at day 24 was the valid result.

Practical standard: Obtain TOC at ≥3 weeks post-treatment completion regardless of which guideline framework you follow. If TOC is positive, assess for re-exposure, partner treatment failure, or treatment failure — and proceed to ID consultation before the next antibiotic course.

The meta-principle: In a pathogen where US clinicians treat blindly — no reportability, no FDA-approved resistance testing, no culture — the correctable variables carry disproportionate clinical weight. Supplement history, counseling quality, and TOC timing are each fully controllable and each directly prevented treatment failure in the case underlying this algorithm.
